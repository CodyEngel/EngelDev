If you are reading this article you probably have some background with
Agile, whether it has been living and breathing it for years or fresh
out of scrum training. One question you probably have is "how the heck
can I be successful with Agile Software Development"? You've come to the
right place, so pull up a chair, grab a drink, and let's talk about the
secret you must know to be truly successful with Agile Software
Development.

## Manifesto for Agile Software Development

It's important to know that there is no **Agile Manifesto**, there is
only a [Manifesto for Agile Software Development](https://agilemanifesto.org). Some of the great thought leaders in the software
industry came together one day some years ago and talked about what they
liked about software development and what they didn't like. Ultimately
they came up with this very to-the-point manifesto.

> We are uncovering better ways of developing\
> software by doing it and helping others do it.\
> Through this work we have come to value:
>
> **Individuals and interactions** over processes and tools\
> **Working software** over comprehensive documentation\
> **Customer collaboration** over contract negotiation\
> **Responding to change** over following a plan
>
> That is, while there is value in the items on\
> the right, we value the items on the left more.

It's easy to get caught up with what is on the left side (in bold text)
and miss what is on the right-hand side. Another way to put it is, the
stuff on the right "processes and tool", "comprehensive documentation",
"contract negotiation", and "following a plan" can still be important,
they are just less important.

Something else to note is that none of this talks about sprints, daily
standup, backlog refinement, sprint demo, retrospective, sprint
planning, TDD, or ***\<insert buzzword here\>***. This just outlines
what has worked the best and what wasn't as important when it came to
software development.

Truthfully you can invent your own framework, my former manager, Sean
Wolter covered Agile in a single sentence which was "Together We Ship
Correct Quality Software". It was unintentionally rank-ordered, so
**Together** is what matters the most, without a team you can't do
anything. Followed by **Ship** because a software engineering team that
doesn't produce software isn't a software engineering team, it's a bunch
of friends hanging out. Next, you have **Correct** because oftentimes
you don't know what correct is until you ship. Last (but still
important) is **Quality**, you'd be surprised how far you can get
without focusing on quality, but nonetheless, it's still important.
While not intentional at the time, this turned into a really easy
framework to follow and stay grounded to *what actually mattered*.

## Principles Behind the Agile Manifesto

Another thing worth calling out before we get to *the secret to agile
software development* is some of
[the principles which the manifesto's signatories felt worth calling out](https://agilemanifesto.org/principles.html) (I avoid saying they
adhere to it because responding to change is more important than
following a plan).

> Our highest priority is to satisfy the customer\
> through early and continuous delivery\
> of valuable software.

There is a reason it's called software and not hardware. Software is
fairly malleable and easy to change assuming it is designed well.
Software isn't rocket science, we are allowed to get the specs wrong,
and we can trust that the customer will tell us when we missed the mark.
So focus on what you think is valuable software, deliver it as quickly
as you can, and then assess the customer feedback. Repeat until your
customers are happy.

> Welcome changing requirements, even late in\
> development. Agile processes harness change for\
> the customer's competitive advantage.

Don't get caught in the [sunk cost fallacy](https://www.behavioraleconomics.com/resources/mini-encyclopedia-of-be/sunk-cost-fallacy/). Eventually, you will work on a project that isn't valuable.
If it took several months to figure out it wasn't valuable, don't spend
several more months finishing it. Keep in mind, none of this says that
the project will be completed in the same amount of time given the new
requirements, it just says to be open to them changing.

> Deliver working software frequently, from a\
> couple of weeks to a couple of months, with a\
> preference to the shorter timescale.

Delivering large projects is always stressful. It is something that
you've received minimal feedback on, you can't imagine how the customer
will break it, and your last release was six months ago so you don't
remember all of the steps required to release the project.

**Shipping less code more often is important.** I say less code because
you can't complete a 2-month project in 2 weeks. What you can do instead
is deliver 8 mini-projects over those 2 months in 2-week increments.

> Business people and developers must work\
> together daily throughout the project.

This doesn't mean that they need to meet daily to talk about status. It
just means that developers should be able to easily talk with folks that
understand the business and customer (as well as the other way around).
If you have to choose between over-communicating and
under-communicating, err on the side of being annoying and
over-communicate.

> Build projects around motivated individuals.\
> Give them the environment and support they need,\
> and trust them to get the job done.

You can't have an effective project when people don't care about it.
Find people that are genuinely excited to solve the problem, get them on
the project, support them when they have questions, but trust that they
will get the work done.

> The most efficient and effective method of\
> conveying information to and within a development\
> team is face-to-face conversation.

This part has become less and less important over the years. Slack works
great for asynchronous questions or low bandwidth items. If it's high
bandwidth discussions then by all means have face-to-face (or
webcam-to-webcam) meetings, but it's not the end all be all for
productive communication.

> Working software is the primary measure of progress.

I'll restate what I said previously in the article: *a software
engineering team that doesn't produce software isn't a software
engineering team, it's a bunch of friends hanging out.* Seriously, the
only way you can measure a software team's success is by the software
they ship.

> Agile processes promote sustainable development.\
> The sponsors, developers, and users should be able\
> to maintain a constant pace indefinitely.

The whole point of having agile processes in place (Scrum, Kanban, XP,
etc) is to make sure that everyone can keep delivering the same amount
of software forever. No one benefits from burnout, so it's worth being
explicit: the point of agile isn't to deliver the same amount of
software faster, it's to deliver less software more frequently.

> Continuous attention to technical excellence\
> and good design enhances agility.

It's very easy to move quickly on a project for a few weeks or months.
Eventually, though poor design and technical mediocrity will slow things
down, what should take 2 minutes to fix will take 2 hours. Technical
excellence and good design can take a little more upfront thought, but
long term you'll be able to reuse that code, and code reuse is a great
way to save time.

> Simplicity — the art of maximizing the amount\
> of work not done — is essential.

Rome wasn't built in a day. If you want software delivered more
frequently it means cutting back on the scope and really focusing on
what matters. You'll find that the simplest solution is often good
enough. If you can deliver one small thing, and deliver it well every
two weeks, your customers will thank you.

> The best architectures, requirements, and designs\
> emerge from self-organizing teams.

Going back to building teams around motivated individuals, you can get
more done by allowing teams to self-organize and figure out what works
best for them. Keep this in mind if you are ever sold a single tool
(Jira) or process (Scrum) to keep all teams aligned.

> At regular intervals, the team reflects on how\
> to become more effective, then tunes and adjusts\
> its behavior accordingly.

Building on the idea of self-organizing teams, you cannot be successful
without reflecting on what worked and what didn't. Oftentimes this can
come in the form or a retrospective, but that's not important. Regularly
meeting with the team to understand what worked and what they would like
to try differently is how you instill real change.

## So What's The Secret?

Okay, I think I've kept you on the hook long enough. The secret is the
process, the tool, and the all-in-one framework doesn't actually matter.
What matters are the people, problem, and product (shippable software).
If you focus on those three things above everything else, I promise you
that you will be successful.

It can become easy to fall into various traps with software engineering.
Sometimes it is using Jira when sticky notes or a spreadsheet would
suffice. Other times it is adhering to various prescribed meetings that
aren't working. If you get value out of the entire team meeting for
stand up then that's great, keep doing it. If you find standup not
helping boost team collaboration or keeping everyone informed then don't
feel bad about canceling it. This goes for every other meeting
prescribed by Scrum by the way, while it can be a great framework it
doesn't work for every team.

Likewise, do you find that certain areas of the software you maintain
are difficult to understand and think it could benefit from
comprehensive documentation? If so, then go for it. In fact, you should
still document as much as you need to in order to keep current and
future co-workers on an even keel. You can make an argument for the
other three items in the manifesto as well.

When it comes down to it, you'd be surprised how much can get done with
a few motivated individuals that enjoy working together and understand
the problem well enough to collaborate with business folks or field
customer questions.
